Interactives I'm using in class - February 1

 Mon 01 February 2016 by Kyle Walker

I've had this blog for about two years now, generally using it to share R code I've written to develop interactive content for my teaching. I'm going to pivot here and do something different. I use lots of interactive visualizations in my courses - some that I develop, and others that I haven't! As such, I'm going to provide periodic round-ups of what I'm using in case others might interested in using the content as well. Click the title links for each content item to view them for yourselves. This past week's topic was cities in North America.

Urban Scratchoff

Innovative and fascinating visualization by Chris Whong that allows users to "scratch off" aerial imagery from 1924 in New York City, revealing imagery from the present day. I'm using Urban Scratchoff to teach about the impact of Robert Moses on New York City, e.g. in the above GIF that shows the central Bronx before and after the construction of the Cross Bronx Expressway.

The Racial Dot Map

This project, from Dustin Cable and the Weldon Cooper Center for Public Service at the University of Virginia, is one of my favorite resources for teaching about the racial landscape of the United States. The above image shows the area of Fort Worth around TCU; the divide between the white population to the west of Granbury Road and the Hispanic population to the east of it is striking, and something that surprises many students.

Visualizing a Changing Region, Block by Block

While a few years old now, this interactive map by Steven Romalewski and CUNY's Center for Urban Research is still one of my favorites. Use the slider to change between a map of race/ethnicity by Census block for several large metropolitan areas between 2000 and 2010. The hue of the block represents the racial or ethnic group that constitutes a plurality there; darker shades represent a greater percentage of that group within the block. I use the map to discuss "demographic inversion" in cities; the above GIF shows how relative African-American concentration declined in central Brooklyn in the 2000s, whereas relative white concentration grew in west Brooklyn during the same period.

Mapping Immigrant America

The Racial Dot Map inspired me to create this project in 2015 to visualize the distribution of immigrants around the United States, and now I use it as a teaching tool in both my visualization/GIS courses and World Regional Geography. I'm using it this week to explore ethnic enclaves in U.S. cities and as context for our discussion on how immigrant economies can work in cities. I plan to work on and release an updated version of the map using the new Mapbox Studio in the months ahead.
